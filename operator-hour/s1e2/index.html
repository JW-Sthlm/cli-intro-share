Recap

Six moves from a messy brief to a deck that lands. We took a real-feeling brief for a fictional partner, Halcyon Advisory, a mid-size advisory firm whose leadership team had to be convinced of one thing in an hour. Then we built the briefing live in front of the room.

Restate. Notes went in. The agent gave back one sharp sentence: what does this room need to believe by the end of the hour? Research. We pulled the Work Trend Index 2026 and public Frontier transformation case studies, the part PowerPoint Copilot can't reach. Model the room. Five leaders, five wants. The CFO needs a number, the CEO needs a story, the Head of People needs a hiring answer. Outline, build, polish. Six slides, one argument each, no bullet lists. Frontier-style HTML, presenter view in a second window, two real fixes against the deck in front of us, then ship.

The argument that held it The leadership team walked in with five separate problems. They are five symptoms of one shift: selling hours stops working when agents do a chunk of the delivery. The trap on screen was a false binary, "add AI to our services" versus "rebuild from scratch." Neither. You re-price the work, not the org chart.

The operator move: the deck is the receipt, the thinking is the win. And the artifact doesn't have to be a deck. The same six-move rhythm builds briefing docs, dashboards, prototypes, automations. Tonight it was a deck.
