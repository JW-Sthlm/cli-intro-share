Prototypes that show, not tell.

A working thing beats a slide about the thing. We took a real customer problem and built a clickable prototype live, in plain language, then handed it over clean so an engineer can take it the rest of the way. By the end we walked up and talked to it.

Recap

You know the meeting. You did the homework, the deck was good, the architecture slide made sense, you even rehearsed. And then the customer leans back and says, looks interesting, send us the deck. That is a no. A polite one, but a no. Not because the idea was bad, but because we asked a room full of busy people to imagine something that doesn't exist yet. That part is on us.

A slide says trust me. A prototype says try it. The reason this matters now is cost. A clickable prototype used to be a week of someone's sprint, so you saved it for the big deals. Now it is twenty minutes, which means it fits inside the prep for a normal Tuesday meeting. Five steps, the same every time: frame the problem, feed it substance, build something clickable, show it, hand it over clean.

The live build. The case was a retail telecom, names changed, constraints kept: an in-store avatar that helps walk-in customers pick a plan, noisy floors, a strict no-personal-data policy, a human for anything binding, and a Thursday deadline. Our whole input was one Teams recap from the call and their plan list as a file. One plain-language prompt, and a clickable kiosk appeared, in their colours, greeting in three languages because the notes asked for three, plan cards pulled straight from their file, and an advisor button because their legal team wanted a human on anything binding. No code written, and none needed.

The shift Twenty minutes ago this was a paragraph in a recap. Now it is a thing the customer can click on Thursday. A slide asks them to imagine. A prototype lets them try.

From mock to real, then clean handover. The terminal demo showed the rest of the real run, compressed: research whether the voice tech survives a noisy floor, a short plan, a two-letter approval, and a deploy to a live URL. Then we walked up to the deployed avatar and asked it for a family plan, and it answered. The last prompt wrote the handover: a README an engineer can follow cold, plus infrastructure-as-code, so their team stands it up in their own subscription. The prototype isn't the end of the conversation. It's the first commit.

What the room asked: do you need a special skill installed to build the clickable mock, or was that just a prompt? (Just a prompt. There is a Web Artifacts Builder skill on disk, and if you build a lot of web things you might want one with your own house style baked in. But for a simple HTML mock like the kiosk, you don't need it. Plain language is enough.)

The operator move: pick one conversation that stalled on a slide, grab the Teams recap from that call, drop it in a folder as recap.md, and run the build prompt. Twenty minutes. One honest note from the room: keep it demo-grade and say so. Fictional data, their shape but never their records. Label it a prototype on screen and out loud. And let it reach production through engineers, a repo and a review, never straight from your demo laptop.

Take-home this week

Turn one stalled conversation into something they can click

Pick a customer or partner conversation that stayed abstract. Grab the Teams recap from that call, drop it in a folder, and run the build prompt from the kit. Block 20 minutes. Show the result to one colleague before you show a customer. Bring what the customer said to E5.
