Prototypes that show, not tell.

A working thing beats a slide about the thing. We take a real customer problem and build a small prototype you can click, live in the room. Then hand it over clean, so an engineer can pick it up and take it the rest of the way.

What we'll cover
  1. Start from the customer problem Frame the one thing the prototype has to prove. Not the whole platform. The single moment that makes a customer lean in.
  2. Research into substance Pull the real context the prototype needs, the part a generic demo can't fake. Their data shape, their workflow, their words.
  3. Build something clickable A working prototype, not a mockup. We describe it in plain language and Copilot builds it while we talk through the choices.
  4. Show, don't tell Demo it the way you'd show a customer. Let them click. A slide asks them to imagine. A prototype lets them try.
  5. Hand it over clean Infrastructure-as-code so an engineer can stand it up and run with it. The prototype isn't a dead end, it's the first commit.
The shift A slide says trust me. A prototype says try it. The fastest way to get a room aligned is to put a working thing in front of them and let them poke at it, instead of describing what it would do one day.
