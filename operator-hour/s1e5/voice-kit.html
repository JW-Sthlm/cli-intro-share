Step 1 · the profile

Build your voice profile do this once

This is the piece that does the work, and it's the part people skip because they think they need a back catalogue of writing. You don't. You've already written your voice down thousands of times in your sent folder and Teams chats. The quickest way to get that writing into a file is Prompt 0 below: one prompt, WorkIQ finds it for you. No manual copy-paste. Then Prompt A builds the profile from whatever landed in that file. Correct anything that's off, and you're done once.

Prompt 0 · gather your writing (WorkIQ) 
Use WorkIQ to find my writing. Pull my 6 to 8 most recently sent emails where I actually wrote something, not forwards or one-liners. Add any longer Teams messages from the last few weeks. Save everything to samples/my-writing.md.

If I have a blog post or article URL, fetch just the text I wrote from it and add it to the same file.

Keep it to my words only. Strip anything confidential, the agent only needs the style, not the content.
No WorkIQ? No problem. Open 6 to 8 sent emails and a few longer Teams messages you wrote. Copy the bodies into samples/my-writing.md, one after another. Add a WinWire or post you were proud of. Same result, just a bit more manual.
Prompt A · build the profile 
Read the writing samples in samples/my-writing.md. These are things I've written:
sent emails, Teams messages, the odd post.

Build me a voice profile as voice-profile.md. Don't summarise the topics.
Pull out HOW I write:

- How I open. Hook style, what I never open with.
- Sentence rhythm. Where I go short, where I run long.
- Words and phrases I use, and ones I'd never use.
- Any tics or signatures worth keeping on purpose.
- Hard rules (for me: zero em dashes, no rule-of-three, no "not just X but Y").

Then show me three lines from my samples that prove each pattern.
I'll correct anything that's off.
It gets sharper over time. The first profile is good. Every time you correct a draft ("I'd never say that", "tighten this"), feed the fix back into voice-profile.md. After a few rounds it's eerily you.
Step 2 · the draft

Real work to a draft the engine

Don't start from a blank page. Start from something that already happened. Drop your notes (a win, an update, a Teams recap, a quick voice memo you transcribed) in the folder next to the profile, and ask for the draft. This example writes a WinWire, but swap that for a Connect update, a vTeam summary, an escalation note, a partner abstract, or a post. Same move.

Prompt B · draft in your voice 
Read win-notes.md and voice-profile.md.

Draft a [WinWire / Connect update / vTeam update / post] from the notes,
in my voice from the profile:

- Open with the outcome, not a wind-up.
- One clear point. Plain language. No filler.
- Keep it the length the channel expects, no padding.
- End with the ask or the next step.

Don't send anything. Hand me the draft to edit.
The last twenty percent is yours. The draft gets you most of the way. Then you add the detail only you were in the room for, cut the line that's almost true, and decide what you actually want to say. That part is the writing, and no model can do it for you.
Step 3 · the pass

Strip the AI tells never skip

Even a good draft carries the robot smell: em dashes, a tidy rule-of-three, "it's not just X, it's Y", every sentence the same length. The humanizer pass catches all of it. Run it on every post before it ships, no exceptions.

Prompt C · the humanizer pass 
Run a humanizer pass on the draft. Find and fix:

- Em dashes. Zero allowed. Use full stops, commas, colons, or parens.
- Rule-of-three lists used as a reflex. Keep one at most.
- "Not just X, but Y" and "it's not X, it's Y" framing.
- Ta-da pivots: "Here's the thing", "Here's what nobody tells you".
- Sentence-length monotony. Vary it. Short, then long.

Show me what you changed and why. Then give me the clean version.
The three rules, always: you read every word before it ships. No invented facts, the numbers and names are yours and real. And run this pass every time. If a reader can tell a machine touched it, it failed.
Make it a system

From sprint to habit level up

The hard version is remembering to do it at all. So you let an engine watch your week, your wins and the threads you're in, and hand you a shortlist of drafts every Monday: the vTeam update, a WinWire or two, the post you keep meaning to write. Then it's a five-minute edit from a draft, not a heroic afternoon. Ask Otto how to set up a lightweight version.
