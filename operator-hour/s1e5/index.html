Sound like yourself, at scale.

WinWires, Connects, vTeam updates, escalations, partner events. So much of the job is writing that has to sound like you, and the blank page eats your week. We teach an agent your voice from emails you've already sent, then point it at real work. Live.

Recap

Look at your week. How much of it is writing? The WinWire after a deal lands, the Connect update, the vTeam note, the escalation, the long follow-up email nobody enjoys sending. The job is full of writing that has to sound like you, and the blank document keeps eating the hours.

AI fills that page in seconds. Here's the catch. You can tell when a machine wrote the text, and lately that reads as lazy. Speed was never the hard part. The empty document was. So we used the agent for the speed and kept the voice for ourselves.

Teach it how you write, not what you wrote. The live build started by pulling a handful of Johan's own sent emails through Work IQ into one file. Then a single question to the agent: how do I actually write? It came back with the tells. Open on tension or a plain fact, never "exciting news". Vary the sentence length. The words to avoid. And never the em dash. That profile is the asset you keep. The emails were just evidence.

The line that stuck The harder part was never the words. It was that empty document. Once the agent owns the first draft, you never start from a blank page again.

Draft from a real win, then strip the robot out. Next we pointed the profile at an actual deal and asked for a WinWire. Running on top was the content-humanizer skill, the same one in the kit, installable in a line. It caught two em dashes and a rule-of-three, flagged them, and rewrote. Under a minute later the draft sat at eighty percent, in the right voice.

The last twenty percent is the whole point. The agent gets you most of the way. Your own fingers do the rest. Skip that step and it won't sound like you, it'll sound like everyone else who used the same tool. We don't write perfectly in real life, so a perfect text isn't the goal. One nobody can tell a machine touched is.

What the room asked: can a few typos make it read as more human? (A couple, yes. Enough to show a person was here, not so many it looks like a problem.) Should the humanizer be a skill or a full agent? (Either works, same outcome. A skill keeps it on tap, an agent lets it act on its own. Your call.)

The operator move: build your voice profile this week, then ship one thing with it. Pull six of your own sent emails, ask the agent how you write, save the profile. Take one real win, draft it in that voice with the humanizer on, and do the final edit yourself. Then post it and tag Johan.

Take-home this week

Build your voice profile, then ship one piece with it

Grab six or eight of your own sent emails or Teams messages. Ask the agent how you write, not what about, and save the profile. Then take one real win from this week and draft it: a WinWire, an update, or a LinkedIn post. Run the humanizer, do the last twenty percent by hand, publish. Block 30 minutes.
